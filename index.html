Welcome to the boardroom.

You are advising ETO management. The old costing system is under pressure. Automation is increasing. Direct labor is shrinking. Customers are complaining. A new machine may make the distortion worse.

Your mission: diagnose the cost-system problem, compare three costing designs, decide how to handle the new machine, and defend a recommendation.

Round 1 - Diagnose why the old system is failing

Select all reasons that help explain why a labor-based burden rate can become misleading in this situation.

Driver mismatch

Overhead is no longer consumed in proportion to direct labor.

Automation and technical support make direct labor a weaker proxy for resource consumption.

Product diversity

Different products use very different combinations of main-room and mechanical-room resources.

A single average rate hides resource-use differences across lots.

Technology shift

More automated testing increases depreciation and machine-related burden.

The denominator, direct labor dollars, shrinks while overhead remains large or grows.

Strategic distortion

Some work may look cheaper or more expensive than it really is.

The system can attract the wrong work and repel the right work.

Red herring

The problem is mainly that direct labor wages are too high.

Wages matter, but the deeper issue is allocation logic.

Red herring

The cost system fails because ETO has no direct materials.

No direct materials is not the core reason the burden allocation is distorted.

Round 3 - Choose the cost system

The board wants a recommendation. Which cost system should ETO use for product/lot costing?

Conservative

Keep the existing one-center labor-based system.

Low disruption, but the distortion continues and may worsen with automation.

Better driver

Adopt the accounting manager's two-center system.

Good improvement: separates admin/technical and machine-hour-driven test-room burden.

Best among given options

Adopt the consultant's three-center system.

Better captures the different economics of main-room and mechanical-room testing.

Ambitious

Create a very detailed ABC system immediately.

Conceptually attractive, but may be too costly and complex for the immediate decision.

Pool everything

Add the machine to the existing one-center pool.

This spreads machine costs to customers who do not use it. Very poor fit.

Main room pool

Add the machine to the main test-room pool.

Better than one pool, but still distorts main-room rates for other customers.

Separate center

Make the new machine a separate cost center.

Costs follow users of the machine, not everyone else.

Best practical answer

Separate cost center, with careful treatment of start-up and low-utilization effects.

Separates the machine and avoids making first-year underuse mechanically drive absurd pricing.

Round 5 - Final board recommendation

Write the recommendation you would defend in class. This is the part students present after the game.

Weak

One-center system; no special machine treatment.

Simple, but ignores the fundamental economics.

Solid

Two-center system now; review after machine acquisition.

Defensible transition, but weaker than three-center for product diversity.

Strong

Three-center system plus separate new-machine center.

Best balance of accuracy, feasibility, and strategic usefulness.

Debrief prompts

  1. Which products were strategically mispriced by the old system?

  4. Should first-year low utilization be passed fully to the first users of the new machine?

Expected strong answer

              
Students should move away from one facilitywide labor-based allocation. The three-center system is stronger than the two-center system because main testing and mechanical testing have different burden economics. The new machine should not be buried in a common pool; it needs separate treatment, with judgment around start-up costs, depreciation, and low first-year utilization.
